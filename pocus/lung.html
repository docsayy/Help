0. Probe Selection: Curvilinear vs Linear

Lung POCUS can be done with several probes, but the curvilinear probe is the workhorse for most adult lung exams. The linear probe is reserved for fine pleural detail and pneumothorax assessment.

  • Curvilinear probe
    • Best for global lung survey, B-lines, A-lines, consolidations, effusions, and PLAPS.
    • Good depth (6‚Äì15 cm) and field of view.
  • Linear probe
    • High resolution of the pleural line.
    • Ideal for subtle pleural irregularities and pneumothorax (sliding vs no sliding, lung point).
Quick rule: Curvilinear for overall lung survey and effusions. Linear when you want maximum pleural detail or are specifically hunting for pneumothorax.

0.1 Lung Scanning Protocol ‚Äì The 8 Lung Zones

A practical way to standardize lung ultrasound is the 8-zone protocol (4 zones per hemithorax). This covers anterior, lateral, and posterior regions where common pathology appears.

Typical Zone Layout

  • Right/Left Zone 1 ‚Äì Anterior-Superior: 2nd‚Äì3rd ICS, mid-clavicular line (MCL). Best for pneumothorax and A-line/B-line pattern.
  • Right/Left Zone 2 ‚Äì Anterior-Inferior: 4th‚Äì5th ICS, MCL. Good for interstitial edema and anterior consolidations.
  • Right/Left Zone 3 ‚Äì Lateral-Superior: anterior axillary line. Picks up lateral interstitial disease and consolidations.
  • Right/Left Zone 4 ‚Äì Lateral-Inferior: mid-axillary line above diaphragm. Good for B-lines, small effusions, and early dependent consolidations.

Posterior basilar zones (if included as an extended protocol or PLAPS) are crucial for:

  • Pleural effusion (free and loculated)
  • Dependent consolidation and atelectasis
  • ARDS-dependent patterns
Scanning sequence tip: Anterior zones ‚Üí pneumothorax vs edema pattern. Lateral zones ‚Üí interstitial vs consolidation. Posterior/PLAPS ‚Üí effusion & pneumonia.

1. Basic Landmarks ‚Äì ‚ÄúBatwing Sign‚Äù

With the probe in an intercostal space, identify two rib shadows with the pleural line between them ‚Äì the classic ‚Äúbatwing sign‚Äù. All other findings (A-lines, B-lines, sliding) are interpreted in relation to this pleural line.

Key idea: Always find ribs and pleural line first. The pleural line is your reference for sliding, A-lines, B-lines, and consolidations.

2. Lung Sliding & Seashore / Barcode Sign

What is lung sliding and why does it occur?

Lung sliding is the shimmering back-and-forth motion of the visceral pleura over the parietal pleura during respiration. It requires:

  • Apposed visceral and parietal pleura
  • Gas-filled lung contacting the chest wall
  • Some tidal ventilation in that region

Seashore Sign vs Barcode/Stratosphere Sign (M-mode)

Place the M-mode cursor through the pleural line:

  • Seashore sign: static chest wall above (horizontal lines) and speckled ‚Äúsand‚Äù below (moving lung) ‚Üí normal sliding.
  • Barcode/stratosphere sign: parallel horizontal lines above and below pleura ‚Üí no sliding (seen in pneumothorax and also in apnea, mainstem intubation, pleural adhesions, or very shallow breathing).
Clinical note: Absent sliding ‚â† automatically pneumothorax. Combine with A/B-line pattern, lung point, and clinical context.

3. A-Lines ‚Äì Reverberation of Normal Aerated Lung

A-lines are horizontal, regularly spaced reverberation artifacts that repeat at equal distances below the pleural line. They occur due to repeated reflection between pleura and probe over highly aerated lung.

A-line predominant pattern usually suggests:

  • Normal aerated lung
  • COPD or asthma (hyperinflated, ‚Äúdry‚Äù lungs)
  • Pneumothorax when combined with absent sliding and possibly lung point
Think A-lines: normal, hyperinflation (COPD/asthma), early/patchy ARDS, or pneumothorax.

4. PLAPS & Curtain Sign

The Posterolateral Alveolar and/or Pleural Syndrome (PLAPS) point is a dependent posterolateral area where effusions, consolidations, and atelectasis commonly collect. Scan the right and left PLAPS points in semirecumbent or supine patients.

Curtain Sign

Over the diaphragm, aerated lung moves down during inspiration and ‚Äúcurtains‚Äù over the liver or spleen. This moving veil is the curtain sign and indicates normally expanding basilar lung.

Loss of curtain sign suggests basal consolidation, pleural effusion, or diaphragmatic dysfunction preventing lung from covering the abdominal organs.

5. B-Lines ‚Äì Why They Happen & How to Interpret Them

What is a B-line and why does it occur?

B-lines are vertical, hyperechoic reverberation artifacts that:

  • Originate at the pleural line
  • Extend to the bottom of the screen without fading
  • Move synchronously with lung sliding
  • Erase underlying A-lines

They represent increased fluid or density in the subpleural interstitium or alveoli (e.g. edema, inflammation, fibrosis).

Interpretation depends on distribution:

  • Isolated basal B-lines: can be normal or represent mild atelectasis/early edema.
  • Focal B-lines: near consolidation or localized pneumonia.
  • Diffuse bilateral B-lines: think cardiogenic pulmonary edema or ARDS ‚Äì differentiate by pattern, pleural line, PLAPS, and clinical context.

6. Consolidation & Subpleural Foci ‚Äì ‚ÄúShred Sign‚Äù

When alveoli fill with fluid, pus, or cells, the lung becomes tissue-like or ‚Äúhepatized.‚Äù On ultrasound this appears as a consolidation with liver-like echotexture.

Shred Sign

The shred sign is an irregular, ‚Äúshredded‚Äù deep border between consolidated and aerated lung, typical of pneumonia.

Think pneumonia when you see consolidation + shred sign + focal B-lines ¬± dynamic air bronchograms, especially in PLAPS zones.

7. Air Bronchograms ‚Äì Dynamic vs Static

Air bronchograms are hyperechoic branching structures within consolidated lung that represent air-filled bronchi.

  • Static air bronchograms: do not move with respiration ‚Üí suggest non-ventilated, collapsed lung (atelectasis).
  • Dynamic air bronchograms: move with inspiration/expiration ‚Üí strongly suggest pneumonia with patent airways.
Key distinction: Dynamic air bronchogram ‚Üí pneumonia; static air bronchogram ‚Üí atelectasis/compression.

8. Pleural Effusion on Ultrasound

Scan posterior or lateral bases and PLAPS points. Pleural effusions appear as anechoic or hypoechoic fluid between the parietal and visceral pleura. Classic signs help confirm and characterize effusions.

8.1 Spine Sign

Normally, the spine is only seen below the diaphragm. When fluid is present in the thorax, the spine can be followed above the diaphragm ‚Äì the spine sign.

8.2 Jellyfish Sign

Partially collapsed lung flapping within surrounding effusion resembles a jellyfish.

8.3 Sinusoid Sign

On M-mode, the visceral pleura/lung oscillates toward and away from the parietal pleura within an effusion, creating a sinusoidal pattern.

8.4 Quad Sign

The effusion is bounded by pleural line, lung line, and two rib shadows, forming a quadrilateral.

8.5 Plankton Sign

Low-amplitude echogenic particles moving within the effusion give a ‚Äúplankton‚Äù appearance, often indicating exudate or complicated fluid.

8.6 Hematocrit Sign

Layering echogenic material with a fluid‚Äìfluid level can indicate hemothorax or highly proteinaceous effusion.

8.7 Loculated Pleural Effusions

Septations or loculations within the pleural space suggest complicated parapneumonic effusion or empyema and influence drainage strategy.

9. Pneumothorax ‚Äì Ultrasound Profile

Ultrasound is more sensitive than CXR for pneumothorax in supine or critically ill patients. Scan anteriorly in supine patients, moving laterally as needed.

Key Findings

  • No lung sliding on B-mode at an anterior position
  • Barcode/stratosphere sign on M-mode
  • A-line pattern (highly aerated lung)
  • Lung point ‚Äì transition where sliding reappears (very specific)

Lung Point

The lung point is where pneumothorax air intermittently gives way to normal lung, so sliding appears and disappears at that probe location. It is highly specific for pneumothorax.

Summary for pneumothorax: No sliding + A-lines + barcode sign suggestive. Lung point found = highly specific.

10. Ultrasound Profile of Pneumonia

Pneumonia often localizes to dependent or posterior lung regions and is well visualized at PLAPS points.

  • Consolidation or hepatization of the lung
  • Subpleural consolidations in peripheral disease
  • Shred sign at the deep border of consolidation
  • B-lines: unilateral or focal; can be bilateral in viral/multifocal disease
  • Dynamic air bronchograms within consolidation
  • Small adjacent pleural effusion common
  • Reduced lung sliding over severely involved pleura
Pneumonia profile: consolidation + shred sign + dynamic air bronchograms ¬± small pleural effusion, usually focal.
